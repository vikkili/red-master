Kitchen Collab - iOS Mobile Service Design

Kitchen Collab is a mobile service based on an iPhone app aiming to help busy people to cook at home more often in a time and money saving way. The Kitchen Collab app empowers customers to select personalized quick recipes, add additional items, and submit a request to pick up the groceries at a convenient place and time. By having the groceries already packed and paid for, zipping by the store on the way home from work is quick and easy. This is a group project Course HCDE 518 User Centered Design.

  • Time: 10/2014 - 12/2014
  • My Role: Research Lead, UX Designer
  • Team Members: Brenda Weitzer, Jennifer Nerad, Kim Lambert
  • Methods & Tools: Survey, Interview, Competitive Analysis, Affinity Diagram, Brainstorm, Sketch, Marvel, Usability Test, Photoshop
  • Design Question: How can we help people who want to cook at home more often than they currently do?

    • Research

    (View Full Research Report)

    Methods

    Through the research phase, we triangulated three research methods to find out the motivation and limitation for cooking home more often and generate design requirements.

    • Survey - We designed an online survey and collected data from 127 participants by convenience sampling.
    • Interview - We conducted nine interviews with a variety of participants who expressed their willingness to be contacted in the survey. During the interviews, we collected audio data and generated 170 written notes. With the consent of some of our participants, we conducted a guided tour after the interview to garner additional information about cooking habits.

      • We built an affinity diagram to analyze the qualitative data from interview and open-ended questions in survey.

    • Competitive Product Analysis - We looked into apps and websites for cooking and time management (since time is the major limitation according to survey results) as well as menu and delivery services.

    Key Findings

    1. Healthy eating and saving money are the main motivations for people who want to cook at home more often.
    2. The dominant reason preventing people from cooking more often is “too busy to cook.”
    3. Easy and fast are the most desired features.
    4. Planning ahead would help.

    Primary Persona

    Refined Design Question: How can we help busy people eat healthier and save money by cooking at home more often?

    Ideate

    Together with my teammates, we brainstormed and sketched 48 ideas. We then narrowed down to three ideas according to our persona and design requirements. We voted and agreed on the final idea.

    Prototype

    Due to the time limitation, we skipped lo-fi and directly jumped into hi-fi prototype, which we later considered as a mistake, because it took huge effort to make modifications on hi-fi prototype during the 5 iterations.

    Play with our initial interactive prototype

    Evaluate & Iterate

    We performed usability test on 5 participants, all of whom finished 3 usability test tasks and answered our follow-up questions. We also prompted them to think aloud during the usability test. Besides empirical evaluation, we also recruited classmates to help us perform cognitive walkthrough. In total, we conducted 5 rounds of iteration.

    View Evaluation Report

    Visual Redesign

    There are still issues unaddressed from the users’ feedback in our final prototype, so I started to redesign the prototype by myself afterwards. Main improvements to be made:

    1. Refine the information architecture to streamline usability.
    2. Add the function to view history.
    3. Visual design should be more aesthetic.

    Oscar Movie Viz - Interactive Movie Explorer

    In this project, we created a set of interactive visualizations allow for exploration of Oscar nominated movies, as well as examining ratings and runtime trends of the Oscar movie dataset. Methodologies included leveraging the User-Centered Design framework to ideate, create, test, and iterate upon visualizations. Final visualizations were produced based on 6 rounds of iteration. This is a group project of course HCDE 511 Information Visualization.

  • Time: 10/2014 - 12/2014
  • My Role: Interaction Designer, Graphic Designer
  • Team Members: Matt Yukubousky, Ankur Agrawal, Monica Caraway
  • Methods & Tools: Survey, Tableau, Usability Test, Guerilla Test
    • Play with our interactive visualization

    Play with Data

    To get the data related to Oscar movies that we needed, we used the official Academy Award database. To collect other details of the movies in that list, we chose to use IMDb and Rotten Tomatoes. This gave us a record for 8832 movies in the dataset. We then played with our data using Tableau and sketched ideas.

    Research

    We collected survey responses from 65 participants. The demographics of our respondents skew towards a younger crowd. All but eight respondents indicated that they were within the 18-34 year old age range. We used the survey as an approach to find out what aspects of movies our users care about and how our visualization tool can help them.

    6 Rounds of Iteration

    I have to say that Tableau is a powerful data analysis and visualization tool which allowed us to quickly iterate and test on real product instead of mockup. We conducted usability test with one participant after each round of iteration. One of the test was guerrilla test which took place in one of the largest specialty movie stores, Scarecrow Video, where we met interesting movie lovers and had fun chatting with them. Improvements are made based on both usability test results and design principles we reflected on.

    Repair Research Study - Design as Inquiry

    This study uses design interventions to examine mechanisms for enabling socially engaging repair practices (e.g., mends on tattered clothing). We followed a research approach that relies on technology design not as an end in itself but as a way to understand social phenomena, from how artists use new technologies to foster insight and action, to how community members engage public space to produce new modes of citizenship. This is a directed research group study led by Professor Daniela Rosner.

  • Time: 09/2014 - 12/2014
  • My Role: UX Researcher
  • Team Members: Yichen Sun, Saba Kawas, Nichole Tilly
  • Methods & Tools: Brainstorming, Workshop Intervention, Observation, Qualitative Analysist

    • Our final paper is submitted to ACM Creativity and Cognition 2015

    Brainstorming

    When we first joined the research group, we had no idea what we are going to do with such a vague topic. We read related papers and conducted 4 brainstorm sessions together in the first month to come up with the research direction.

    Design Interventions - Workshop Design

    We employed the research through design methods to examine the activities of the mending community. We designed each workshop using an iterative design process in which our participants provided feedback and allowed us to iterate on the designs. In total we conducted 6 workshops.

      Each workshop set out to achieve two goals:
    • Understand the interplay between design and invention for these practitioners.
    • Examine how sewing interventions could reveal insights into people’s relationships with their objects.

      Workshop structure:
    1. A discussion of participants’ definitions of repair.
    2. Recalling narratives related to the items.
    3. Pairing up with another participant.
    4. Brainstorming ideas of a combined item using the two existing items.
    5. Show and tell.

    Analysis

    Throughout the workshops, we observed and took notes. We also collected video and audio data with the consent of 5 groups of participants for detailed analysis. We first generated key thematics based on the field notes and our memory of presence. Then we referred to videos to look for vignettes that reveal the themes.

    Evaluation of Children’s Mobile Apps

    - Undergraduate Thesis

    This research focused on evaluating the interaction design of general tasks, including selecting, saving and deleting, in typical children’s mobile apps. We conducted field study and performed qualitative analysis to synthesize the interaction behavior of children aged from 4 to 6 based on video data collected from participant observation. We also derived corresponding design principles as a reference for designers in the industry. This is an individual undergrad thesis I did in Peking University.

  • Time: 01/2014 - 06/2014
  • Methods & Tools: Participant Observation, Qualitative Analysis, Usability Test
  • Advisor: Jun Wang

    • View the thesis paper in Chinese.
    The long abstract Evaluating the Interaction Design of General Tasks in Children’s Mobile Apps is accepted for the iConference 2015 poster session.

    Research Design

    We selected 4 iPad apps with typical interaction designs in the general task modules we are examining and designed evaluation tasks for each module.

    We recruited 12 children age from 4 to 6 by convenience sampling and conducted participant observation during the whole evaluation.

    Key Findings

    By analyzing the video data, we compared children’s performance in each task and found some consistency and trends. The following discussion should not be viewed as the results of a comprehensive scientific study, but rather as exciting indications that a lot of design details in children’s mobile apps could be improved in terms of usability.
